History

Floods are the most common natural disaster in India. Here is a brief history of notable floods in India:
1923, Lucknow: One of the earliest recorded floods in Lucknow's history, caused by heavy rainfall in the monsoon season, which made the Gomti River overflow its banks.
October 1943, Madras (now Chennai): The city experienced its worst flood due to excessive rains that lasted for 6 days and overflowed Coovum and the Adyar rivers.
17 October 1960, Lucknow: The flood was caused by the overflow of the Gomti River after heavy rainfall in the Himalayan region. It submerged almost half of the city under several feet of water.
8 September 1971, Lucknow: The flood was caused by two breaches in the embankment of the Gomti River due to heavy rainfall in the monsoon season.
Between 1980 and 2017, India experienced 235 floods, which led to 126,286 deaths and affected 1.93 billion people. The economic losses due to floods stood at a humongous $58.7 billion. Floods in India account for over 40 percent of the deaths out of all natural disasters.

What to do?

Dos

1. Stay Informed: Pay attention to weather forecasts and flood warnings from official sources like the National Weather Service. Sign up for emergency alerts if available in your area.
2. Have an Emergency Plan: Create a family emergency plan that includes a communication strategy and evacuation routes. Share this plan with your family members.
3. Prepare an Emergency Kit: Assemble an emergency kit with essential supplies such as non-perishable food, water, clothing, flashlights, batteries, a first aid kit, and important documents.
4. Elevate Electrical Systems: Elevate electrical outlets, switches, and circuit breakers to at least one foot above the expected flood level in your area.
5. Install Sump Pumps: If you live in a flood-prone area, consider installing a sump pump in your basement to prevent water damage.
6. Raise Appliances: Elevate appliances like water heaters and furnaces to reduce the risk of damage from flooding.
7. Secure Hazardous Materials: Store hazardous materials (like chemicals or pesticides) in elevated and secure locations to prevent contamination during flooding.
8. Move Valuables to Higher Ground: If possible, move valuable possessions to a higher floor or a safe, elevated location in your home.
9.Evacuate When Advised: When authorities issue an evacuation order, leave your home immediately. Follow designated evacuation routes and go to a safe shelter.
10. Turn Off Utilities: If time permits and it's safe to do so, turn off gas, electricity, and water before leaving your home.

Don'ts

1. Don't Ignore Warnings: Take flood warnings seriously. Even minor flooding can cause significant damage and pose risks to your safety.
2. Don't Drive Through Flooded Areas: Avoid driving or walking through flooded streets and underpasses. Just a few inches of fast-moving water can sweep you off your feet or stall your vehicle.
3. Don't Wait to Evacuate: If an evacuation order is issued, don't wait until the last minute to leave. Delaying your departure can put your life at risk.
4. Don't Touch Electrical Equipment: Avoid contact with electrical equipment, appliances, and outlets if you are in a flooded area or if you suspect water damage.
5. Don't Enter Flooded Buildings: Do not enter your home or any other building that has been flooded until it has been declared safe by authorities.
6. Don't Drink Floodwater: Floodwater can be contaminated with sewage and other pollutants. Do not use it for drinking, cooking, or personal hygiene.
7. Don't Ignore Emergency Alerts: Stay updated on weather and emergency alerts through official channels. Avoid spreading unverified information or rumors.
8. Don't Isolate Yourself: Stay connected with friends and family, especially if you are in an evacuation shelter or a safe location.
9. Don't Ignore Signs of Hypothermia: In cold floodwater, be aware of the signs of hypothermia and take steps to warm up if you experience them.
